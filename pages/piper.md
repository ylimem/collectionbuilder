# Childhood 

Childhood is a keyword for Black Louisiana that attempts to encapsulate not just the simple definition of an age range, but also encapsulates the experience of learning and growing as a child. Childhood looked very different between races. Childhood for enslaved children meant starting formal work assignments as early as eleven for physical labor or younger for domestic activities [^1]. While white children could indulge in education and pleasure activities, enslaved children were forced to either be companions – but never friends – of white children or wait to work in the fields with adults. 

The documents under the keyword mention rebellious childhood activities, as in “A Child, Cesar, Vandalizes Property,” and the protection of children by family, as in the pieces related to Charlotte or Catherina. What these documents fail to show are the typical, day-to-day lives of enslaved children. What might they have done to have fun? How did they cope with their experience? Did they get to play and be mischievous? These children were viewed as assets to enslavers because they could be reared in the institution of slavery, yet they were still just kids. Many children during slavery were the result of sexual violence against Black women, meaning they could be a slave in their father’s home [^2]. How might this information also taint the childhood of enslaved children? We invite you to consider these questions as you read through the documents.
