# Death 

Death is a keyword for Black Louisiana that encapsulates varied definitions. Death can be violent, death can be agonizing, but also, death can be freedom. Death is not just defined by the action of dying, but what it may mean. This keyword also encapsulates murder, which, during slavery, meant the killing of the enslaved and enslavers at the hands of either group. Some enslaved people became executioners, becoming agents of death within the institution of slavery, while some perished within it. These are just a few definitions of death, but death was also influenced by African religions and religious practices, in which death looked a lot different than would be viewed in the Americas or now. 

In the document, “At Long Last, Charlotte is Free,” Charlotte gains her freedom through the will and testament of her enslaver. Death served as a way for her to gain her freedom. Death also provided freedom for enslaved people because, as the poet George Moses Horton exclaims, the grave is the “The only refuge for the slave, Who mourns for liberty.”[^1] In a period where death and murder were common place through lynchings, the slave trade, and illness, death also provided a way for resistance. Enslaved people were considered property so death became a method of resistance as it "disenfranchised" enslavers. We invite you to consider these nuances of death during slavery in these documents. 

[^1]: George Horton. SLAVERY - By a Carolinian Slave Named George Horton. 1828. Poem. https://nationalhumanitiescenter.org/pds/maai/emancipation/text2/hortonslavery.pdf.
